title: Entry Types
---

Every entry in the Encyclopedia of Rhetoric belongs to one of four formats, each calibrated to the scope and complexity of its subject. A figure who appears across dozens of other entries may warrant a multi-section Article, while a narrow technical term may be served best by a concise Identification. Understanding these formats helps you gauge how deeply an entry treats its subject — and when to follow cross-references to find fuller coverage elsewhere.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Identifications" icon="tag">
    The shortest entries in the encyclopedia. An Identification gives you a precise, economical definition of a rhetorical term, figure, or concept — typically a few sentences to a short paragraph. These are your first stop when you encounter an unfamiliar word in a longer entry. Identifications rarely include a bibliography, but they may carry **See Also** links that point you toward Notes or Essays for deeper context.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Notes" icon="file-lines">
    A step up in length and depth, Notes provide elaborated explanations of topics that require more than a definition. Expect a page or two of discussion covering the term's origin, its development across rhetorical traditions, and examples of its use. Notes frequently include a bibliography listing the primary scholarship and may contain **See Also** links to closely related entries.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Essays" icon="book-open">
    Essays offer in-depth explorations of major subjects — rhetorical movements, influential texts, or concepts whose significance spans multiple periods and disciplines. Running to several pages, an Essay situates its subject historically, surveys its theoretical dimensions, and traces its influence. You will almost always find a bibliography and **See Also** links at the close of an Essay.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Articles" icon="book-bookmark">
    The most comprehensive format in the encyclopedia. Articles provide full scholarly treatments that illuminate rhetoric's art and methodology, drawing on primary sources, intellectual history, and disciplinary debate. The entry on Aristotle, for instance, traces his biography, his philosophical system, and his lasting influence on rhetorical theory across nearly three millennia. Articles consistently include a bibliography and **See Also** cross-references.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## How Length and Format Relate

Entry format is determined by the editorial judgment of the original contributors and reflects the subject's breadth within rhetorical studies, not a strict word-count rule. As a general orientation:

| Format | Typical length | Bibliography | See Also |
|||
| Identification | A few sentences – 1 paragraph | Rare | Sometimes |
| Note | 1–2 pages | Often | Often |
| Essay | Several pages | Yes | Yes |
| Article | Extended, multi-section | Yes | Yes |

<Note>
  Longer entries — Essays and Articles in particular — almost always close with a collapsible **Bibliography** section and a set of **See Also** pill links. Use these to trace a concept across multiple entries and build a richer picture of the topic.
</Note>

## Navigating Between Formats

Because the encyclopedia spans 274 entries of varying depth, you will often move between formats as you research. A practical path might look like this: you encounter an unfamiliar term in an Article, follow a **See Also** link to its Identification for a quick definition, and then open the related Essay for historical context. The encyclopedia is designed to reward that kind of lateral movement — no single entry is meant to stand entirely alone.
