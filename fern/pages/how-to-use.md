title: How To Use
---

The Encyclopedia of Rhetoric and Composition is built for both quick lookups and deep exploration. You can arrive with a specific term in mind and find a precise definition in seconds, or you can follow cross-references and knowledge-graph connections for an hour and emerge with a genuine map of a rhetorical tradition. This page walks you through the four core ways to work with the encyclopedia.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Navigate or Search">
    Start from wherever feels natural. If you know the term or figure you want, press **⌘K** (macOS) or **Ctrl+K** (Windows/Linux) to open full-text search and type your query — results appear instantly across all 274 articles.

    If you prefer to browse, open the **Browse Overview** from the sidebar to see all entries organized alphabetically across 23 letter sections. You can scan by letter, skim entry titles, or filter by entry type.

    <Tip>
      The search shortcut **⌘K / Ctrl+K** works from any page in the encyclopedia. You do not need to navigate to a dedicated search page first.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Read an Entry">
    Each entry opens with a definition or framing statement, followed by a discussion that places the term, figure, or concept in its historical and rhetorical context. Longer entries include section headings to help you jump to the part most relevant to your needs.

    Where available, you will also find:
    - A **bibliography** of primary and secondary sources for further reading
    - **Cross-references** to related entries (see Step 3)
    - An **entry type label** indicating whether you are reading an Identification, Note, Essay, or Article
  </Step>

  <Step title="Follow Cross-References">
    Rhetoric is a web of interconnected ideas, and the encyclopedia reflects that. Within entries, you will find references to related articles — follow them to trace a concept across time periods, compare competing schools of thought, or move from a broad term to a specific figure who exemplifies it.

    You can also use the **interactive knowledge graph** to visualize how entries relate to one another. Open the graph from any entry page to see which articles share conceptual neighbors, then click any node to navigate directly to that entry.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Use the AI Assistant">
    The encyclopedia includes an AI chat assistant powered by Claude 3.5 Sonnet. You can ask it questions in plain language — "What is the difference between ethos and persona?" or "Which entries discuss Kenneth Burke?" — and it will respond with answers grounded in the encyclopedia's content, citing specific articles.

    The AI assistant is best used for:
    - Synthesizing ideas **across multiple entries**
    - Getting oriented on an unfamiliar topic **before reading deeply**
    - Asking follow-up questions that a static index cannot answer

    <Note>
      Open the AI assistant at any time with the chat icon in the navigation bar. Like search, it is accessible from every page in the encyclopedia.
    </Note>
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Understanding Entry Types

Not all entries are the same length or depth, and knowing the difference helps you set expectations before you read.

<Accordion title="Identifications">
  The shortest entry type — typically one to three paragraphs. Identifications provide a concise definition of a rhetorical term, concept, or figure, giving you enough context to understand a reference or decide whether you want to read further. Think of them as the encyclopedia's dictionary layer.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Notes">
  Elaborated explanations that go beyond a bare definition to situate a topic within rhetorical history or scholarship. Notes often address why a term or figure matters, how usage has evolved, and where scholarly disagreement exists.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Essays">
  In-depth explorations of major subjects — typically several pages — that trace historical development, survey competing interpretations, and situate the topic within broader rhetorical and humanistic debates. Essays are the right starting point for a research project.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Articles">
  The most comprehensive entry type, offering a full scholarly treatment of rhetoric's most significant terms, movements, and methodologies. Articles include detailed bibliographies and extensive cross-references, making them the encyclopedia's primary resource for advanced research.
</Accordion>
