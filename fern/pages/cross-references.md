No rhetorical term, figure, or concept lives in isolation. Anaphora presupposes an understanding of repetition schemes; Aristotle's *Rhetoric* cannot be read without reference to ethos, logos, and pathos; an essay on the Sophists opens onto questions of epistemology and pedagogy that ramify across dozens of other entries. The encyclopedia's **See Also** system makes these connections explicit. At the bottom of many entries — and occasionally embedded within the body text of longer Articles — you will find a set of pill-shaped links pointing you toward related entries that deepen, complicate, or extend what you have just read.

## How See Also Links Work

See Also links appear as labeled pills in a dedicated **See Also** section, typically just above the Bibliography at the close of an entry. Each pill is a direct hyperlink to another entry in the encyclopedia. The relationships they encode are editorially curated: contributors chose them to reflect genuine conceptual kinship, not merely alphabetical proximity.

## Following Cross-References to Deepen Understanding

Cross-references are most powerful when you treat them as an invitation to build a **conceptual cluster** around a topic rather than a simple lookup chain. Here is an example of how that works in practice.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Example: Anaphora and its neighbors">
    The entry on **Anaphora** — the repetition of the same word or phrase at the opening of successive clauses — carries See Also links to related schemes of repetition, such as **Epistrophe** (repetition at the *end* of successive clauses) and **Symploce** (the combination of anaphora and epistrophe, with repetition at both ends). Reading these three entries together gives you a precise map of how classical rhetoricians categorized positional repetition, and why each term names a meaningfully different effect.

    Following the same cluster further, you might find links to **Parallelism**, **Isocolon**, or **Antithesis** — entries that illuminate the broader structural logic behind schemes of balanced or repeated phrasing. What began as a single definition expands into a coherent understanding of an entire corner of rhetorical style.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Example: Aristotle and philosophical context">
    The Article on **Aristotle** connects outward to entries on **Ethos**, **Logos**, **Pathos**, **Enthymeme**, and **Epideictic** — the major concepts his *Rhetoric* introduced or systematized. It also links to figures like **Isocrates** and **Plato**, whose rival schools shaped Aristotle's project. Following these cross-references turns a biographical entry into an intellectual map of fourth-century BCE rhetorical thought.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Tips for Using Cross-References Effectively

<Steps>
  <Step title="Read the entry first">
    Let the main entry establish the concept before you follow any links. Cross-references are most illuminating when you already have a foothold in the topic — they help you locate *how* the new entry differs from or extends what you just read.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Follow one link at a time">
    Resist the temptation to open every See Also link at once. Choose the pill whose label you find most unfamiliar or most directly relevant to your research question, read that entry, and then reassess which link to follow next.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Use the sidebar to retrace your steps">
    The sidebar maintains the full alphabetical entry list at all times. If you have followed several cross-references and want to return to your starting point, the sidebar is a reliable anchor.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  Cross-references are directional: the entry on Anaphora may link to Epistrophe, but Epistrophe may carry its own distinct set of See Also links rather than simply mirroring Anaphora's. Each entry's cross-references reflect that contributor's editorial judgment about the most meaningful relationships from *that* entry's perspective.
</Note>
