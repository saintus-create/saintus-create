The simplest way to locate an entry you already know is to browse alphabetically. The Encyclopedia of Rhetoric organises all 274 articles in letter-by-letter order, mirroring the convention of printed scholarly reference works. Each sidebar section corresponds to one letter of the alphabet — click the letter grouping that matches your term and scan down to the entry you need.

## How Alphabetical Navigation Works

Entries are sequenced by the first significant word in the title. A theorist entry such as _Barthes, Roland (1915–1980)_ files under **B** for Barthes, not **R** for Roland. Compound terms such as _African-American Rhetoric_ appear under **A**, and entries with Roman-numeral century labels — _Eighteenth-Century Rhetoric_, _Twentieth-Century Rhetoric_ — are filed under **E** and **T** respectively.

<Note>
  The current edition has no entries for letters **J** and **X**. All other letters from A through Y have at least one article.
</Note>

## Letters with the Richest Coverage

The letters **A**, **C**, **P**, and **R** contain the largest concentrations of entries — reflecting the density of classical terminology (most of which is Latin or Greek in origin), the abundance of rhetorical figures beginning with those letters, and the number of theorist entries whose surnames cluster in the early alphabet. If you are browsing without a specific target, those sections reward exploration.

<Tip>
  Don't want to scroll? Use the **search bar** at the top of the page. Typing even a partial term — "chiasm," "etho," "nineteenth" — instantly surfaces matching entries without requiring you to identify the correct letter section first.
</Tip>

## Entry Formats

Within each letter section, you will encounter four kinds of entries:

- **Identifications** — a paragraph or two defining a term
- **Notes** — a page or more of elaborated explanation
- **Essays** — multi-section treatments of major topics
- **Articles** — full scholarly discussions with bibliography and cross-references

Cross-references at the end of most entries use the abbreviation _q.v._ (Latin _quod vide_, "which see") to point you toward related articles elsewhere in the encyclopedia.
