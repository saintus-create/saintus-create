Rhetoric has been defined, disputed, and reinvented by an extraordinary succession of thinkers across 2,500 years. The encyclopedia includes dedicated entries for more than thirty individual theorists, from the reputed founder of the discipline in fifth-century Sicily to twentieth-century scholars who brought rhetoric into dialogue with linguistics, psychoanalysis, feminism, and deconstruction. The entries below are organised roughly by period; each name links to a full article that covers the theorist's life, major works, and contribution to the discipline.

<Note>
  Most theorist entries include a **See Also** section listing related encyclopedia articles. If you arrive at a theorist entry and want to explore the broader intellectual context, those cross-references are the fastest route to connected topics.
</Note>



## Roman Rhetoricians

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Cicero" icon="columns">
    Rome's greatest orator and most prolific rhetorical theorist. His _De Inventione_, _De Oratore_, _Brutus_, and _Orator_ synthesised and extended the Greek tradition, while his speeches established the stylistic model for Latin prose that Renaissance humanists sought to recover.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Quintilian" icon="book">
    Author of the _Institutio Oratoria_ (c. 95 CE), the most comprehensive Latin handbook on rhetorical education ever written. Quintilian's vision of the _vir bonus dicendi peritus_ — the good man speaking well — tied rhetorical excellence inseparably to moral character.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>



## Enlightenment and Nineteenth-Century Theorists

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Campbell, George (and Adam Smith)" icon="scales-balanced">
    George Campbell's _Philosophy of Rhetoric_ (1776) grounded persuasion in faculty psychology and Humean empiricism. Alongside Adam Smith's _Lectures on Rhetoric and Belles Lettres_, Campbell's work defined the dominant paradigm of eighteenth-century British and North American rhetorical theory.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Whately, Richard" icon="gavel">
    Anglican bishop and logician whose _Elements of Rhetoric_ (1828) returned the discipline to Aristotelian argumentation after a century of belletristic drift. His treatment of presumption and burden of proof remains influential in informal logic and debate theory.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Bentham, Jeremy (1748–1832)" icon="balance-scale">
    Utilitarian philosopher whose _Book of Fallacies_ and theory of _universal grammar_ brought linguistic analysis to bear on the rhetoric of political argument and legislation.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Emerson, Ralph Waldo (1803–1882)" icon="sun">
    American essayist and philosopher whose lectures and essays treated rhetoric as the art of making ideas felt — and whose lifelong engagement with Hugh Blair's _Lectures_ shaped his eventual break with belletristic tradition.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## How Theorist Entries Are Structured

Each theorist article in the encyclopedia follows a consistent pattern: it opens with a brief biographical identification, then describes the theorist's principal works and ideas, assesses their influence on subsequent rhetoric and related fields, and closes with a bibliography of primary and secondary sources. The **See Also** cross-references at the end of each entry link to related conceptual entries — so an entry on Aristotle will point toward **Enthymeme**, **Ethos**, **Logos**, **Pathos**, and **Stasis Theory**, while an entry on Burke will connect to **Dramatism**, **Identification**, **Consubstantiality**, and **Logology**.
