Rhetoric has a longer continuous history than almost any other intellectual discipline. From the law courts of fifth-century Syracuse to the networked discourse of the information age, scholars have returned again and again to the same core questions: How does language persuade? What makes communication effective and ethical? What is the relationship between words, knowledge, and power? The encyclopedia's 274 entries collectively trace that 2,500-year conversation. The overview below organises relevant articles by historical period so you can follow rhetoric's development in sequence or dip into any era that interests you.



## Medieval Period (c. 400 – 1400 CE)

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, rhetoric was transmitted and transformed within the institutions of the Christian church. The sermon became the dominant rhetorical genre; the _ars praedicandi_ (art of preaching) replaced the _ars oratoria_ as the most widely taught rhetorical form. Scholars such as Bede integrated classical precepts with scriptural exegesis, and the universities of the high Middle Ages embedded rhetoric within the _trivium_ alongside grammar and logic. Scholasticism reconfigured the relationship between rhetoric and dialectic in ways that would shape debate long into the Renaissance.

## Enlightenment and Eighteenth-Century Rhetoric (c. 1700 – 1830)

The eighteenth century produced one of the most sustained and influential reconfigurations of rhetorical theory in the discipline's history. Scottish rhetoricians — above all Hugh Blair, George Campbell, and Adam Smith — reoriented rhetoric around faculty psychology, the sublime, taste, and the emerging genre of belles lettres. Campbell's _Philosophy of Rhetoric_ (1776) attempted to ground persuasion in the empirical science of human nature. Whately's _Elements of Rhetoric_ (1828) returned to Aristotelian argumentation. The period also saw rhetoric migrate from the training of orators into the teaching of written composition — a shift with profound consequences for education on both sides of the Atlantic.
