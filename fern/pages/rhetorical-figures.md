Rhetorical figures are the building blocks of eloquent, persuasive language. The encyclopedia covers dozens of individual figures drawn from the classical tradition — Greek and Latin devices named, defined, and illustrated by rhetoricians from Aristotle and Quintilian to Renaissance handbooks such as Henry Peacham's _Garden of Eloquence_. Whether you are analysing a political speech, a literary text, or everyday persuasion, you will find the relevant figure documented here with definition, examples, and scholarly context.

## Figures by Category

Rhetorical figures are traditionally divided into **schemes** (patterns of arrangement that depart from ordinary word order or repetition) and **tropes** (figures that shift or transfer the conventional meaning of words). Schemes are further subdivided by the type of structural pattern they exploit.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Figures of Repetition" icon="repeat">
    Figures of repetition achieve emphasis, rhythm, and emotional force by returning to the same word, phrase, or sound at strategic positions within a sentence, clause, or passage.

    | Figure | Brief Definition |
    |  |
    | **Anaphora** | Repetition of the same word or phrase at the opening of successive clauses — "I came, I saw, I conquered." |
    | **Epiphora** | Repetition of the same word or words at the _end_ of successive clauses — the mirror image of anaphora. |
    | **Anadiplosis** | The last word of one clause becomes the first word of the next, creating a chain effect. |
    | **Epanalepsis** | A clause or sentence ends with the same word with which it began. |
    | **Symploce** | The combination of anaphora and epiphora; the same word opens and another closes successive clauses. |
    | **Epizeuxis** | Immediate repetition of a word without intervening words, for emphasis: "never, never, never." |
    | **Diacope** | Repetition of a word with one or more words between the repeated instances. |
    | **Ploce** | Repetition of a word in a different sense or relation within the same sentence. |
    | **Gradatio** | A figure in which successive clauses build on the concluding word of the previous one, creating an ascending or descending series. |
    | **Polyptoton** | Repetition of a word in different grammatical forms or cases within a passage. |
    | **Accumulatio** | The gathering of numerous points, words, or arguments in a series for cumulative effect. |
    | **Inclusio** | The framing of a passage by repeating at its close the same word or phrase used at its opening. |
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Figures of Contrast" icon="arrows-left-right">
    Figures of contrast set opposing ideas, structures, or images against one another to sharpen meaning, highlight differences, and generate memorable phrasing.

    | Figure | Brief Definition |
    |  |
    | **Antithesis** | The conjoining or contrasting of ideas, typically in parallel grammatical form — "A man should be mourned at his birth, not at his death." |
    | **Chiasmus** | An ABBA pattern of mirror inversion in which words, grammatical structures, or ideas are reversed in successive clauses. |
    | **Antimetabole** | Repetition of words in reverse order, often for pointed contrast: "Ask not what your country can do for you…" |
    | **Isocolon** | A series of parallel clauses of equal or near-equal length, balancing weight and rhythm. |
    | **Dissociation** | The rhetorical separation of a concept normally treated as unified into two distinct categories. |
    | **Diaphora** | Repetition of a name, first as a proper noun and then as a common noun, to exploit a distinction in meaning. |
    | **Antanaclasis** | Repetition of a word in two different senses within the same passage, exploiting ambiguity. |
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Figures of Omission and Interruption" icon="scissors">
    Figures of omission achieve compression, dramatic pause, or stylistic variety by leaving out expected grammatical elements or breaking off a thought before its conclusion.

    | Figure | Brief Definition |
    |  |
    | **Asyndeton** | The omission of expected conjunctions between words, phrases, or clauses: "I came, I saw, I conquered." |
    | **Polysyndeton** | The use of _more_ conjunctions than ordinary usage demands, slowing the pace and emphasising each item. |
    | **Aposiopesis** | A sudden breaking off of speech — leaving a sentence unfinished, conveying strong emotion or implied threat. |
    | **Parenthesis** | The insertion of an explanatory or qualifying phrase into a sentence in a position that interrupts its normal syntactic flow. |
    | **Anacoluthon** | An unexpected shift in grammatical construction mid-sentence, mimicking spontaneous speech or interrupted thought. |
    | **Preteritio** | Calling attention to something by claiming to pass over it: "I will say nothing of his dishonesty…" |
    | **Anapodoton** | A figure in which one of the two expected elements of a conditional or comparative construction is omitted. |
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Tropes" icon="wand-magic-sparkles">
    Tropes transfer the conventional meaning of a word to something new. They are the most semantically rich of the figures and have attracted sustained attention from both classical rhetoricians and modern theorists of language.

    | Figure | Brief Definition |
    |  |
    | **Metaphor** | An implicit comparison that directs attention to particular aspects of a subject by identifying it with something from a different domain. |
    | **Synecdoche** | Substituting a part for the whole, or the whole for a part: "All hands on deck." |
    | **Metonymy** | The substitution of one word for another that is closely associated with it: "the Crown" for the monarchy. |
    | **Irony** | Language that means the opposite — or something different from — its literal sense, often signalled by tone or context. |
    | **Hyperbole** | Deliberate overstatement for emphasis or rhetorical effect: "I've told you a million times." |
    | **Catachresis** | The apparently inappropriate substitution of one word for another — a "necessary misuse" in Quintilian's phrase. |
    | **Anthimeria** | The substitution of one part of speech for another, as when a noun is used as a verb. |
    | **Metalepsis** | A trope in which a word or phrase is used at a further remove from its original sense, typically involving a chain of figurative leaps. |
    | **Periphrasis** | The substitution of a descriptive phrase for a proper name or simple expression. |
    | **Hypallage** | The transference of an epithet from its proper subject to a related one. |
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## On the Classical Taxonomy

The four-part scheme above reflects the organisation found in major classical handbooks — particularly the _Rhetorica ad Herennium_, Cicero's _De Oratore_, and Quintilian's _Institutio Oratoria_ — and adapted by Renaissance systematisers such as Peter Ramus and Henry Peacham. Modern treatments sometimes collapse or reorder these categories, and the encyclopedia's individual entries note where definitions have shifted across historical periods. If you want to understand those debates in depth, the entries on **Style**, **Tropes**, and **Figures of Speech (Wilson, Thomas)** are the best starting points.
