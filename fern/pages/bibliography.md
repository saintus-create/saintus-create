Many entries in the Encyclopedia of Rhetoric close with a curated bibliography — a list of the primary scholarly works that informed the entry and that a reader might consult for deeper study. These bibliographies were assembled by the original contributors and reflect the state of rhetorical scholarship as of the **1996 edition**, edited by Theresa Enos and published by Routledge as part of the *Garland Reference Library of the Humanities*. They are an authoritative starting point for researching a topic, even if the field has continued to develop in the decades since.

## Accessing a Bibliography

Bibliographies do not appear inline in the body of an entry. Instead, you will find them tucked behind a collapsible section at the very bottom of the page, beneath the main text and any **See Also** links.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Scroll to the bottom of the entry">
    Navigate past the entry's main discussion and any See Also pills until you reach the end of the page.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Locate the Bibliography section">
    Look for the **Bibliography** label — a small, uppercase divider line that marks the start of the collapsible section.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Click to expand">
    Click anywhere on the Bibliography header row. The section will expand to reveal the full list of citations. Click again to collapse it.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  Not every entry includes a bibliography. Identifications — the encyclopedia's shortest entries — typically do not. Bibliographies appear most consistently in Notes, Essays, and Articles, where the depth of treatment warrants a reading list.
</Note>

## How Citations Are Formatted

Citations in the encyclopedia follow the author–date–title–publisher convention standard in humanities scholarship. A typical entry looks like this:

```
Nash, Walter. Rhetoric: The Wit of Persuasion. Oxford: Basil Blackwell, 1989.

van Dijk, Teun A. "Principles of Critical Discourse Analysis." Discourse & Society 4 (1993): 249–83.
```

Each citation includes:

- **Author** — Last name first, followed by first name or initials
- **Date** — Year of publication, placed after the title for journal articles or at the end for books
- **Title** — Book titles and journal names are italicised; article titles appear in quotation marks
- **Publisher** — City of publication followed by the publisher's name for books; journal volume and page range for articles

<Tip>
  If you copy a citation for your own notes, double-check the publication details against a library catalog. A small number of citations in the 1996 edition contain minor typographical variations in publisher names or dates.
</Tip>

## Working with a 1996 Bibliography

Because the encyclopedia was compiled in the mid-1990s, its bibliographies naturally do not include scholarship published after that date. The field of rhetoric has grown considerably since then — new critical editions, digital humanities approaches, and expanded work in areas like visual rhetoric and computational discourse analysis have all appeared since publication.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What the 1996 bibliography covers well">
    The bibliographies are especially strong for classical and early modern rhetorical theory, mid-twentieth-century revival scholarship, and foundational works in composition studies, discourse analysis, and argumentation theory. If your research touches on Aristotle, Cicero, Burke, or the epistemic rhetoric movement, the citations in these entries will point you to the essential primary and secondary literature.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where you may need to supplement">
    Post-1996 developments — including work in digital rhetoric, feminist and postcolonial rhetorical theory, multimodal communication, and corpus-based stylistics — will not appear in the bibliographies. For these areas, use the AI assistant or a library database to identify more recent sources.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Tip>
  The encyclopedia's AI assistant can help you find scholarship published after 1996. Try asking it: *"What are recent works on anaphora in discourse analysis?"* or *"Can you suggest post-2000 scholarship on Aristotle's rhetoric?"* — it will surface relevant titles beyond what the original bibliography contains.
</Tip>
